समाधिBot: A katydid is a five-legged insect that’s kind of like a grasshopper —

Miles: It’s six-legged, that one just lost its leg. Sometimes their back legs fall off.

समाधिBot: Why would their back legs fall off?

Miles: I don’t know, they’re just not attached that well.

India: They do it for defense, like if a wasp egg was clinging on it, you know —

Miles: Uhh, no they don’t, they don’t really do it for defense —

India: You know how those sea star things —

Miles: No, it’s not like that. They sometimes get their leg snagged… and then their leg falls off.

India: And they’re not injured at all…

समाधिBot: What do these guys eat?

Miles: Leaves.

समाधिBot: Leaves exclusively? They wouldn’t eat an aphid if one came along?

Miles: Grass… things like that. Things like grasshoppers eat, they seem to have a grasshopper diet. Except they seem to like leaves more. And sometimes they bite people.

समाधिBot: Really? So they would eat an aphid?

Miles: Yeah maybe. And look, they have tiny little antennae right there… and there… [pointing to the sides of its mouth]

India: Is it like a Komodo dragon that smells with its tongue?

Miles: No.

India: So it smells with its nose?

समाधिBot: It also has a weird plate-like thing on the tip of its nose.

Miles: This is what it does… [gestures]

समाधिBot: Oh that’s it’s upper lip? What’s behind that?

Miles: Underneath there’s… how am I gonna do this… [makes sideways chewing gestures]

समाधिBot: Ah!

Miles: Like that… mandibles. And it looks just like a leaf. Its wings look just like a leaf. They even have veins to go with it.

Taj: Hey guys let’s just delete this one. This one isn’t good. This interview isn’t good —

समाधिBot: It’s never too late! Things can always turn around —

Taj: How much more minutes? How much more minutes?

समाधिBot: Um… we don’t know. You never know how long an interview is gonna go. But I edit heavily…

Miles: So we get out all of the poo talk —

समाधिBot: Yes, any mention of poo gets removed.

India: I know one thing about them: they’re like praying mantises, they lick their legs all the time. And if you look closely at the skin it looks like jelly.

समाधिBot: It looks like bubbly green candy jelly.

Taj: And someone might want to eat this one.

समाधिBot: Oh yeah, and its little hand! It’s got a hook at the end, what does it do with its hook?

Miles: I think it just sits around with it.

You Say sɪˈkeɪdə and I’ll Say sɪˈkɑːdə

Miles’ files
16 July 2015

समाधिBot: What do you know about cicadas?

India: Miles, what do you know about cicadas?

Miles: What do you know about cicadas?

India: They make ear splitting sounds?

समाधिBot: Why do they do that?

India: I dunno, to attract a mate or something?

समाधिBot: Is it a female trying to attract a male, or a male trying to attract a female?

India: I guess a male?

Miles: They usually make the earsplitting noises while they’re eating. I don’t know why, but they drink sap at the same time.

Taj: India doesn’t know about any cicadas.

India: And they’ve got giant eyeballs.

समाधिBot: Can they see well?

Miles: They have pretty good eyesight. They’re not like a dragonfly or a mantis shrimp. Mantis shrimps can see about seventeen times better than humans.

समाधिBot: Really?!! Mantis shrimps?

Miles: Yes. They also sit in holes, and when divers poke at them they smash off their fingers.

समाधिBot: Good lawd!

Miles: They don’t bite them off, it’s just the force of it, they go like that [gestures] and their pincer travels out at about 500 miles per hour.

समाधिBot: Do these guys have a mouth?

Miles: No, they have a little sucky thing squished against their neck, and then they up-stretch it and stab it into a tree, and suck its blood, basically.

समाधिBot: Could they do that to a person?

Miles: No.

समाधिBot: Are you sure? I was a little freaked out by this little guy when I was taking his picture. ‘These any relation to a locust?

Miles: I don’t really know, but I’m pretty sure that they’re basically a giant spittle bug.

समाधिBot: Don’t those spittle bugs have a seventh appendage —

Miles: A gear. A naturally forming gear.

समाधिBot: A naturally forming gear — am I thinking of a spittle bug?

Miles: Yes.

समाधिBot: These don’t have anything like that.

Miles: Yes but they do fly in much the same way. And they have the same sort of shape.

समाधिBot: They have kind of like a hammerhead shark head.

Miles: Yeah, and they look like they have mustaches.

समाधिBot: Do they have a mustache?

Miles: Mmm, I dunno, I think it’s just antennae right below their nose and their mouth.

India: Have you seen how the head looks kind of like a hammerhead shark’s head?

Miles: Yeah we were just talking about that. And right at the bottom of the wings they’re bright green.

समाधिBot: Where do cicadas overwinter? Are they eggs then? Are they grubs?

Miles: I’m not really sure. The ones that come out every seventeen years —

समाधिBot: Isn’t that locusts?

Miles: No cicadas, it’s a variety of cicada. Those ones grow insanely slowly.

समाधिBot: And they’re underground for all that time, right?

Miles: Mmm-hmm.

The Origin of Geeses

Samadhiblot
13 July 2015

India: Why is the egg so big? Usually goose eggs are much smaller than that.

समाधिBot: It’s kind of the storybook golden egg. I was thinking about that a lot while I was drawing it. It’s not realistic is it?

India: Mmm…

समाधिBot: But the goose is always sitting on a preposterously large golden egg.

India: Like the queen ant is always sitting there laying eggs.

समाधिBot: Have you ever drawn a goose?

India: Yes… a baby goose.

समाधिBot: Do you know what a baby goose is called?

India: … a gooseling?

समाधिBot: A gosling.

India: Bother…

समाधिBot: I once got chased by a goose. Ever been chased by a goose?

India: Is it scary?

समाधिBot: It is!

India: Well… they are big honking gooses…

समाधिBot: They lift their heads up high and spread their wings and hiss, and all of a sudden they go from being something not much different from a duck or a chicken… to this terrifying once-was-a-dinosaur thing chasing after you.

India: But I’ve been chased by a bull before, in India.

समाधिBot: Well that’s a lot scarier. How’d you get chased by a bull?

India: Remember? We got a bit too close. That was scary.

समाधिBot: I don’t think that was a bull though.

India: What was it?

समाधिBot: It was a cow. Yeah the bulls are like a ton of rippling muscle.

India: It went like this… remember?

समाधिBot: It was doing that thing with its hoof…

India: The hoofy thing?

समाधिBot: Mm-hmm. Do you know the story of the goose laying the golden egg?

India: Jack and the Beanstalk? Uh… yeah. I’ve seen that show before.

समाधिBot: Is there any other story of a goose laying a golden egg, or is Jack and the Beanstalk the only one?

India: I guess it’s the only one. There’s a Puss ’n Boots version of it, and there’s a mommy goose that’s the size of a house, or a Titanoboa. Early birds had teeth, daddy.

समाधिBot: Yeah?

India: Yeah. They just had tiny little nubbin teeth. And early birds looked like penguins.

समाधिBot: Really?!!

India: Yeah, rrreally big fluffy penguins.

समाधिBot: [Laughs] I thought early birds were dinosaurs.

India: They were, but they were big, fluffy looking penguins. [Makes crashing sounds like giant dino-penguin feet stomping] And there’s this kind with flippers.

समाधिBot: I wonder if that’s how the birds survived when other dinosaurs did not. Maybe there was some kind of… what’s the word…

India: Safety ground? A safe coral spot?

समाधिBot: An intervention! Maybe there was an intervention where they said, these other dinosaurs, the Spinosauruses and whatnot, things with spikes and clubs… we’re going to let them burn. But these big soft, fluffy penguin-like dinosaurs are just too cute to let get wiped out.

India: Oh by the way, they were this big. [gestures]

समाधिBot: About… four feet high?

India: Mm-hmm, that’s a really big fluffy penguin. The kind with flippers is only this big.

समाधिBot: You know if we could clone one of those big fluffy dino-penguins, we could make a lot of money…

India: Yeah… they’re really fluffy… they’re this fluffy —

समाधिBot: Wow, mostly fluff?

India: Yeah, the females can get to this fluffy.

समाधिBot: Really? Were they just adorably fluffy, or were they also cold blooded killers?

India: At that time they were just vegetarians. They were just curious, they would come here, eat a bit, go to another spot, eat a bit.

समाधिBot: That sounds nice…

India: Yeah. They didn’t really have to worry about swimming, because of all their fluff, it just made them float.

समाधिBot: Could they fly then?

India: Nope. At that time they could kind of fly if it got really windy, then they would just go up in the air —

समाधिBot: Like a tumbleweed!

India: Yeah, like a tumbleweed! Anyway, how did you draw the G into the goose?

समाधिBot: I drew the letter G very lightly first, and then I looked for a goose to draw around it. So I could see the body of a goose, mostly inside the letter G, and I could imagine the head of the goose at the end of the top of it.

India: Those big fluffy penguins, they had lonnng pointy beaks. Yeah like that…

समाधिBot: Was it a specific species of big fluffy penguins, or there were lots of different kinds?

India: It was two kinds of them. I don’t know the names of them, but I called the one that we were first talking about the tumbleweed. The next one is kind of skinny, so I call it the naked bird.

समाधिBot: The naked bird?

India: Yeah, it doesn’t really have many feathers. It just has big feathers it walks around in. They’re funny.

समाधिBot: Alright… penguin interview!

India: And goose.

समाधिBot: Oh yeah, goose.

Fan Mail From Some Flounder

Samadhiblot
3 June 2015

समाधिBot: That’s a chalkboard drawing for the letter F. Do you know what it is?

India: A flounder?

समाधिBot: It is a flounder! It’s a flounder that is breaching the water. You don’t see that everyday.

India: No, you don’t see that everyday with normal flounders. I’ve seen them before but I haven’t seen them doing that.

समाधिBot: That might be a little bit of artistic license, but I figured it was better than a flounder on the seabed with an F painted on it.

India: Mmm-hmm.

समाधिBot: Have you ever drawn a flounder?

India: Yeah, I’ve drawn one before.

समाधिBot: You have? Is it in your notebook?

India: No it was on a piece of paper…

Taj: Where is it now?

समाधिBot: Have you ever seen a flounder jump out of the water, Taj?

Taj: I don’t because I don’t know what a flounder looks like.

समाधिBot: They’re the funny, fflat fffish with both eyes on one side of their head, instead of on either side like most fish.

Taj: Mmm…

India: There’s this giant flounder in the sea, and there’s this lonnng fish — you know those really long fishes? Those flounders are so big, and they’re one of the most gentle flounders. They’re a bit on the rough side, so they attack those long fishes a lot, because they’re the big meat-eating flounders.

And especially in the mating season, flounders and those fishes, they don’t really like where they’re mating if it’s near the other flounders or other fish. So they potentially fight off any other things.

समाधिBot: Ffighting ffflounders?

India: Yeah. The way they fight is pretty funny. They get their sides, and swim swim swim —

Taj: I think Eli has a flounder. It’s pretty smooth. Pretty skinny.

समाधिBot: In his fish tank? Huh!

India: I didn’t know Eli has a fish tank.

Taj: It was a gold fish tank. But there was not a goldfish in it.

समाधिBot: Can you see the letter F in the fish?

India: Yes it’s that big green thing… there’s the F. That’s a very pretty flounder.

समाधिBot: Yeah I don’t think most flounders are ffancy ffflounders like that.

India: Most of them are grayish… yeah they’re kind of olivy colored with gray spots on them usually.

समाधिBot: I saw some that had blue spots on them. I’m not positive those were flounders, but there was one that stood out from the rest as being more decorative. Most of them seem like they just try to blend in with the bottom of the ocean.

India: Especially the really small ones try to do that. Because way bigger flounders, instead of the mommy flounders, some of those flounders like to shoo them away. So they hide because they don’t really like being shooed around, because their fins really haven’t evolved into big powerful fins. They’re like tiny little flippers.

So they just stay around, and if any food drops down from the sea, like if a seabird dropped a bit of seaweed for its nest, then they just go for it. Yeah all they do is just lie around watching birds fly over them, and big sharks and whales.

समाधिBot: Oh and they would have a good view because both their eyes are on top of their head.

India: Mmm-hmm.

समाधिBot: So it’s not so bad to be a flounder.

India: Yes.

Taj: Why does that thing look like a hand?

समाधिBot: It’s a fin. I could have just drawn a fin. That’s an F word too. But I guess a fish… a fish has fins… a fish conveys more F-ness.

India: Yeah. You could have drawn tiny little F’s on the fins. Why did I not think of that?
